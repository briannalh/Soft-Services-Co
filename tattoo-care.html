Peanut butter and jelly, Charli xcx and A.G. Cook, body art and body care. All are good, even great on their own. But when they come together, they become more than the sum of their parts.

About a third of adults in America have at least one tattoo, according to a 2023 Pew Research survey; the odds are that if you don’t already have one, you’re thinking about your first. The process is intense on the skin. A needle pokes down into the outermost cutaneous layer, deposits a little bit of ink, and repeats this process a few more hundred or thousand times.

Proper body care not only helps to scaffold the skin’s healing process, but it also ensures the tattoo itself comes out looking bright, clear, and smooth—just as the artist and subject intend.
Step 0: Before the Tat
It’s generally recommended that all exfoliation be suspended about 10–14 days before the tattoo appointment, until further notice (we will provide further notice). Skin too raw and new is tough to tattoo. Don’t even worry about shaving: Your tattoo artist will take care of that on the table.
All you need to do is drink water, get plenty of sleep, and moisturize your body. Not only will this make healing easier, but it’ll make for a better canvas, too.

Step 2: 2-3 days post-tat
A tattoo takes around four to six weeks to fully heal, by which we mean the skin itself. This is your body’s natural regenerative process, and you can help it along—and give yourself some relief—by moisturizing the area up to three times a day. “One of the biggest mistakes you can make after getting a tattoo is to let it dry up,” a dermatologist told Allure. Dry skin can flake and crack, which can change the outcome of the tattoo, or invite infection.

Step 3: 3-4 weeks post-tat
How are things looking? The answer probably varies depending on who you are and what body art you got, but it generally takes about three weeks for a tattoo to heal, and you may notice some residual dead skin or scabbing. Perhaps you’re just tired of not exfoliating. Well, there’s good news and bad news.

The good news: It’s time to exfoliate. The lightest of active ingredients can be reintegrated into your routine, and can help the skin surrounding the tattoo turn over. Opt for urea—an exfoliating ingredient that helps shed dead skin cells very gently, while also imparting moisture.

The bad news: If you’re a Certified Buffer® , you may need to wait a little longer before you go to town. As one dermatologist put it recently to New York magazine, it’s best to avoid harsh
physical scrubs and acids for the first three months of the healing process. Tattoo ink is placed in the deep dermis layer, and while topical products like exfoliants work on the outer epidermal layer, it’s wise to exercise caution for a little while longer.

You’ve waited long enough. Now that your tattoo has fully healed, you may return to scrubbing or treating with abandon. Regular exfoliation can actually improve the look of tattoos over time, by removing the dead skin that collects over top and dulls them.
